The Cost to Us - Take Up Your Cross

Scripture Reading

Matthew 16:24-26 (ESV)

Then Jesus told his disciples, "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me. For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake will find it. For what will it profit a man if he gains the whole world and forfeits his soul? Or what shall a man give in return for his soul?"

Devotional

After calling us to deny ourselves, Jesus gives a second requirement for discipleship: "take up his cross."

In Jesus's day, this image would have been shocking and disturbing. The cross was an instrument of execution, not a piece of jewelry or a metaphor for minor inconveniences. When someone "took up their cross," they were walking to their death. There was only one destination for a person carrying a cross: Golgotha, the place of execution.

So when Jesus calls us to "take up your cross," He's calling us to a kind of death—a death to our old way of life, our selfish ambitions, our worldly values. The Apostle Paul understood this when he wrote, "I die every day" (1 Corinthians 15:31). Taking up your cross is a daily decision to put to death whatever belongs to your earthly nature (Colossians 3:5).

What might this look like in practical terms? For some, it might mean putting to death a specific sin that has entangled you—an addiction, a habit of dishonesty, a pattern of gossip or criticism. For others, it might mean dying to good things that have taken too central a place in your life—career ambitions, financial security, comfort, or even family expectations.

Taking up your cross might also involve embracing suffering for Christ's sake. Jesus was clear that following Him would sometimes lead to persecution, rejection, and hardship. "If they persecuted me, they will also persecute you," He told His disciples (John 15:20). Taking up your cross means being willing to endure these hardships rather than compromising your faith to avoid them.

This is countercultural in a world that prioritizes comfort, convenience, and self-preservation. Our natural instinct is to avoid pain, maximize pleasure, and protect ourselves at all costs. But Jesus calls us to a different path—one that embraces sacrifice for the sake of something greater.

Why would anyone choose this path? Jesus gives us the answer: "For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake will find it." There's a paradox here. By trying to preserve our lives—our comfort, our security, our autonomy— we actually lose the life we were created for. But by surrendering our lives to Christ— embracing the death of our old selves—we discover true life.

Jesus reinforces this with two penetrating questions: "For what will it profit a man if he gains the whole world and forfeits his soul? Or what shall a man give in return for his soul?" He's asking us to consider the value proposition. Even if we could gain everything this world offers—all its wealth, pleasure, power, and acclaim—what would it profit us if we lost our souls in the process? And once lost, what could we possibly offer to buy our souls back?

The implication is clear: your soul is worth more than the whole world. The eternal outweighs the temporal. What Christ offers—forgiveness, purpose, relationship with God, eternal life—is of infinitely greater value than what the world offers.

Today, consider what it means for you to take up your cross. What in your life needs to die so that Christ might live more fully in you? What suffering might you need to embrace rather than avoid for the sake of following Jesus? And how does the promise of finding true life motivate you to lose your life for Christ's sake?

The image of taking up our cross is one of the most powerful in all of Scripture. In Jesus' day, carrying a cross meant facing certain death. It was a symbol of extreme suffering and sacrifice.

For us today, taking up our cross means being willing to face the difficulties and challenges that come with following Jesus. It means:

  • Being willing to face opposition for our faith
  • Making sacrifices for the sake of others
  • Enduring hardships for the kingdom
  • Putting God's priorities above our own
  • Remaining faithful even when it's difficult

Taking up our cross is not about seeking suffering for its own sake, but about being willing to endure whatever it takes to follow Jesus faithfully. It's about prioritizing His kingdom over our comfort, His will over our preferences, and His glory over our own.

The good news is that when we take up our cross, we don't do it alone. Jesus has already carried the ultimate cross for us, and He walks with us through our own challenges. His strength is made perfect in our weakness.

Reflection Questions

  1. What specific aspects of your old self do you need to "put to death" as you take up your cross?
  2. How have you experienced the paradox that losing your life for Christ leads to finding true life?
  3. What worldly gains are you most tempted to pursue at the risk of forfeiting your soul?

Prayer

Lord Jesus, thank You for modeling what it means to take up a cross. You embraced suffering and death for the joy set before You. Give me courage to follow Your example— to die daily to my old self and embrace whatever suffering comes from following You faithfully. Forgive me for the times I've chosen comfort over obedience, self-preservation over sacrifice. Help me to value my soul more than the whole world and to remember that what You offer is infinitely more valuable than what the world offers. In Your name I pray, Amen.

Today's Application

Identify one specific area where you need to "take up your cross" this week. It might be confronting a persistent sin, embracing a difficult obedience, or accepting suffering that comes from standing firm in your faith. Write down this commitment and share it with a trusted friend who can pray for you and hold you accountable.
